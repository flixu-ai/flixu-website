# Defining Post-Editing

In the modern architecture of B2B localization, **Post-Editing** represents the definitive dividing line between unacceptable, generic machine output and pristine, culturally resonant enterprise software. It is the structured process wherein a professional linguist receives an algorithmic first draft generated by a translation engine and meticulously edits the text to ensure absolute semantic, grammatical, and emotional perfection.

To comprehend the necessity of Post-Editing, one must acknowledge the current limitations of pure artificial intelligence. While Neural Machine Translation (NMT) and Large Language Models (LLMs) possess staggering capabilities for raw linguistic fluency, they fundamentally lack true lived human experience. An AI does not instinctively know what metaphor effectively motivates a Parisian procurement officer, nor does it inherently understand the subtle, unspoken visual hierarchy of a German cybersecurity dashboard.

The computer calculates probability; the human validates the culture.

## Service Level Agreements: Light vs. Full

Within the global Language Service Provider (LSP) ecosystem, the contractual scope of Post-Editing is strictly defined by two distinct Service Level Agreements (SLAs), driven by the customer's budget and the visibility of the content.

### 1. Light Post-Editing (LPE)

Light Post-Editing is a highly restrictive, low-budget editing framework. The linguist is explicitly instructed to execute the bare minimum amount of cognitive labor required to make the text technically comprehensible. If the machine generated an awkward but grammatically acceptable sentence, the linguist does not touch it. Their mandate is exclusively to fix critical errors: missing negative clauses, egregious mistranslations, or offensive phrasing.
_Operational Use Case:_ Internal corporate memos, large legacy knowledge bases, or low-tier e-commerce product reviews.

### 2. Full Post-Editing (FPE)

Full Post-Editing is the uncompromising pursuit of publication-grade perfection. The linguist is paid a premium rate to substantially restructure the algorithmic output. They interrogate every noun to ensure it matches the corporate glossary, adjust the verb pacing to guarantee brand alignment, and rewrite clumsy machine phrasing until the final product reads exactly as if a native human had translated it from scratch.
_Operational Use Case:_ Core SaaS User Interfaces (UI), enterprise legal compliance documents, and high-visibility digital marketing assets.

## The Cognitive Shift: From Typist to Quality Assurance

Historically, the Post-Editing workflow (specifically referred to as MTPE—Machine Translation Post-Editing) was deeply loathed by professional linguists. Legacy statistical engines produced such fragmented, fragmented gibberish that "editing" the text was actually more mentally exhausting than simply deleting the machine output and translating the entire document manually from a blank screen.

The advent of **Contextual AI Orchestration** has fundamentally inverted this dynamic.

Platforms like Flixu do not hand the human editor a generic, blind machine draft. By injecting the corporate Active Glossary and the target Brand Voice Profile directly into the LLM prompt _before_ generation, the AI outputs a culturally native, highly accurate draft on the very first pass.

Consequently, the physical act of "editing" heavily diminishes. The human linguist rarely has to fix broken grammar or incorrect terminology. Instead, their role elevates to **Linguistic Quality Assurance (LQA)**. They act as high-level narrative directors, spending their cognitive energy exclusively on polishing the emotional resonance of the transcreation, allowing enterprise teams to achieve flawless global launches at unprecedented velocity and scale.
